  • Cortex

    On Cortex, it's super interesting to hear how Grey and Myke give insights into the tools – both software and hardware – they use to complete their work.

    I really love their episodes on AI and its art as well as their yearly themes episodes, where they take a look both the way they've been approaching their work overt the last year, and how they want to focus on the next.

  • Rock, Paper, Scissors

    In Rock, Paper, Scissors you get to weave your way through a maze of probability, rampaging with rock, slicing with scissors, and plotting with paper, to conquer unimaginable odds in surprisingly few games.

    When playing for the first time, I urge you to be honest about whether you were truly victorious or not. If you lose, you must click the lose link, for to corrupt your initial expierience with cheater malice would truly ruin your expierience of the video. And, most importantly, if you lose the first time don't just click off the video. There is a dedicated end card link for if you are defeated, which, if clicked, opens up a whole new world.

    Now go! Venture through the unlikely undertaking and see if you will be the luckiest person to ever live.

    I mean, you probably won't, but it's worth a shot at least.

  • The surprising pattern of color names

    The surprising pattern of color names is a video from Vox exploring the way that cultures all across the world seem to give colours their names in pretty much the same order, even through there is no obvious reason why some colours are more important to name than others.

    To me, the introduction of this video is just spectacular. It immediately does a great job at getting the viewer intrigued, and then swiftly presents the topic at hand in a way which gets you curious to learn more.

    The animation and sound are both done great as well, which helps elevate the experience.
